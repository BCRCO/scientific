1.1 Knowledge, Definition and Creation

Since ancient times, philosophers wished to know not only what knowledge is, but how and from where does it arise. The research on the origin of knowledge has been one of the major topics of a discipline called epistemology. The word epistemology is derived from the Greek word episteme (knowledge) and the word logos (study). So the word is currently used to denote “the study of knowledge” or “justified belief”. To answer what knowledge is from an epistemological point of view, you must first understand that there is not only one kind of knowledge. Although epistemology deals with different types of knowledge, such as knowing how to do something (e.g. playing an instrument), knowing something or somebody, it is more concerned with propositional knowledge. A proposition is something that can be expressed by a declarative sentence, and in this sentence the clause “that” is used: “She knows that a piano is a musical instrument”; “he knows that Moscow is the capital of Russia”. Propositional knowledge presents the general schema “subject knows that proposition”. In this last example, the subject is “he” and the proposition is “Moscow is the capital of Russia”. Propositional knowledge is the knowledge in a factual sense. This will help you understand where knowledge comes from and what are the necessary conditions for a subject to actually know or have knowledge of a certain proposition. What are the necessary conditions for you to know that Moscow is the capital of Russia? There are several approaches to answer this. The traditional approach has three core elements to explain these conditions. If you do not believe that Moscow is the capital of Russia, then, this becomes a proposition that you don´t know. Knowledge requires belief. But not all beliefs constitute knowledge. You can be mistaken in what you believe. Therefore, knowledge requires truth. But these two criteria do not suffice to build up knowledge. It must be acquired the right way (through solid evidence and sound reasoning). A lucky guess do not constitute knowledge. A belief must be justified, and that’s the third ingredient to build up knowledge, justification. These three criteria build up the basic definition of knowledge, the “justified true belief” (Steup, 2005).

You can see how new cell phones with new or improved characteristics reach the market, or that a drug to heal certain disease has been discovered. But, are you able to differentiate whether these accomplishments are achievements in science or technology? Science and technology have many similitudes, but they do differ. The term knowledge is related to the term science. According to the Merriam-Webster Dictionary (2014), the word science comes from the Latin word scientia, which means “having knowledge” and from the verb scire, which means “to know” or “to be instructed”. Science is the pursuit of knowledge for its own sake while technology is the application of this scientific knowledge to the practical aims of human life. To make it easier to grasp, technology is the application of the scientific knowledge in everyday life. Differences between science and technology

1.3 Classification of Sciences. How many sciences do exist? Can you mention some sciences besides biology, chemistry, physics, social, and medical sciences? How are sciences classified? One of the most important classifications was done by the American philosopher Charles S. Peirce (1839-1914) in 1903. Peirce proposed three classes of scientific inquiry, three great kingdoms of science (Burch, 2010):

Science of discovery Its sole aim is to discover new truth for its own sake. Within this group you can find mathematics, philosophy (cenoscopy), and special sciences (idioscopy), which are further divided into all the particular and special sciences, like physics, psychology, etcetera. Science of review Sets forth the science of discovery, so that it may serve any purpose. It deals with issues such as the history of science, the classification of sciences and the synthetic forms of philosophy; it deals with the arranging of the results of discovery. Practical science Discovers truth for the sake of some definite purpose. It comprises a form of scientific inquiry intended to fulfill genuine human needs, e.g. engineering, medical sciences, etcetera.

Peirce’s classification of sciences is one of the most noted classifications ever done. His classification, however, can only be valid if it is brought under constant revisions, since it is not able to comprise all the sciences that exist today and will exist in the future.

2.1 The Scientific Research Process There is no exclusive guideline on how to develop a research process. However, it must be agreed that research starts with an idea, a concern or a necessity born from the researcher’s curiosity; this moves the researcher to endeavor and enlighten the unknown. So, the research process has certain steps whose purpose is to conceive the research idea.

  • Conceive the research idea. The researcher thinks about a problem in nature, science or society that needs to be solved.
  • Topic selection. The initial idea may be very wide and could encompass a lot of peripheral topics which may or may not be of interest for the researcher. In this step, a thorough bibliographic research must be done to narrow the original idea to the topic of interest.
  • Establishment of the research’s objectives, questions and hypothesis. Once the researcher has refined his question and investigated the current knowledge on the topic through the bibliographic research, he may define the research’s objectives, questions (what is not known) and hypothesis (what the result is expected to be).
  • Construction of the theoretical framework. The construction of the research’s theoretical context is aimed to extensively introduce the current situation of the problem in question. It includes a deep research in the bibliography.
  • Design of the methodological strategy. In this step the ways to measure what has been conceptually defined are proposed: methods, techniques and instruments that will help you through your investigation. What you are going to do with the gathered information will also be defined here.
  • Data gathering and interpretation. The data is interpreted and compared with the original research’s approaches.
  • Report elaboration. The report, in which the objectives, questions and hypothesis are solved, is presented. This must contain the conclusions and results of the research that was done.

2.2 Research Methodology Every approach and perspective involved in the research process as a whole is called a methodology. Methodologies intend to justify and answer questions of the research’s details, such as why and how; what data was collected and how it was analyzed. There are two main research philosophies, which encompass the different kinds of research methodologies, the positivistic and the phenomenological.

  • Positivistic perspective. Also called quantitative, objectivist, scientific or experimentalist approach. This is the approach commonly used in science. Seeks to measure and evaluate phenomena and also explain them rationally.
  • Phenomenological perspective. Also called qualitative, subjectivist, humanistic or interpretative approach. It is implemented in the description and explanation of events from the perspectives of the people who are the research’s subjects. Research methodologies can be encompassed in the previous perspectives. There is no particular way to choose a given methodology; it rather depends on the researcher’s own interest, assumption and intentions (Neville, 2007).

2.3 Three Approaches Research can be approached in the following ways:

  • Quantitative / Qualitative: that is if numerical data will be collected or other subjective information, aspects, values and perceptions will be analyzed.
  • Basic / Applied: that means if knowledge will be improved without any particular applied purpose, or if the research is designed from the start to apply its findings.
  • Deductive / Inductive: if general theories will lead to specific or particular situations by deduction, or if particular situations will be used to make or infer broad general ideas or theories (Neville, 2007). Now you will focus on the first approach, “Quantitative or Qualitative”. Science methods are classified in these two branches or approaches. Quantitative. The emphasis of qualitative techniques is measuring, collecting and analyzing numerical data (e.g. range, scale, frequency) of phenomena. This type of research is detailed, structured, precise and data can be easily collected and statistically treated. These techniques are usually more expensive, since they require measuring devices or equipment. They are harder to design initially, and they are not used for deep study of the phenomenon, but only to get information on some research variables. Qualitative. 3.1 Formulation of a Question The interest on a topic is the starting point in every scientific research (Sabariego and Bisquerra, 2004). You will choose certain research topic depending on your own interests and expertise, but to continue with the research process, you must conceive a problem. The research problem is the materialization of the topic of interest and, unlike the latter, is researchable. Kerlinger (1976) proposed the following guide criteria to formulate a research problem:

  • The implied phenomenon must be observable.
  • It must handle at least two variables, in order to make its description more precise.
  • Every person should understand what is being proposed. The problem must not leave unanswered doubts.
  • It must be delimited. The most common way of presenting and setting a problem is in the form of a question. According to Cardona (2002), a well-defined problem will help the researcher to define what is being studied (the research objectives), with whom (subjects), and how is the problem studied (variables).

The following are some research questions that can be done depending on the kind of research:

Type of Research Questions
Descriptive What is the phenomenon? How is it called? What is the relevance of the phenomenon? How often does it occur? What are its characteristics? Does it show always in one way or varies? Can it be analyzed in parts, sections or factors?
Exploratory Is there background information of this research topic? What characteristics does it show? What is really happening? What produces this phenomenon? Why is it necessary to experiment on it?
Analytical Is there any theoretical or practical explanation of it? Is it measurable? How does it evolve? How does it behave? Why does it exist? Where does it come from? What is the meaning?
Predictive What happens if you change or intervene in the phenomenon? Is it possible to modify it? What would happen if you modify it? Can it be controlled? Can we make it happen?

4.1 Literature Selection and Review Before starting the construction of the theoretical framework, a deep search for the relevant bibliographical sources for the research topic must be done. These can be classified as follows (Sampieri, 1991):

  • Primary sources: books, scientific papers, specialized magazines, documentaries, etcetera. They are the direct source of information.
  • Secondary sources: lists of primary sources, which can include a descriptive summary of the primary source. Example: internet searches.
  • Tertiary sources: Documents that summarize names or titles of magazines and other periodical publications, newsletters, conferences, companies, and so on, that can give hints on where to look for sources.

Literature can be found either in specialized libraries, internet, databases or magazines. Internet is a good source to look for secondary or tertiary sources, from which primary sources can be later obtained. It is recommended that the literature is at least five years old. Another information source is to consult specialists and other professionals in the area, which can give valuable advice on your research intentions. It is a good idea to also know who are the most important researchers in your study field, which aspects have they researched, and if there is any researcher who has already done research in a similar context as yours.

4.2 Information Extraction It is important to note if all the literature found meets your research needs. To identify and extract information for your research correctly, there are important recommendations and strategies to follow.

  • It is crucial to comprehend what is written and being read. Take a good time for reading.
  • How do you determine in which sources a deep immersion is needed, and how to discard useless information? Before dealing deeply with academic texts, you can start reviewing its abstract, the index of contents, the conclusions or its cited sources. By doing this, you can decide if the aspects handled in the source are related with your research problem.
  • When reading academic texts, more than only decodifying the written text or copying, it is important to comprehend it.
  • You must be able to identify diverse opinions regarding a topic, to weight them, and to build up new perspectives from them.
  • Make charts and concept maps of what you read. There are different ways to read texts as described by Mateos (2009), according to the comprehension level:
Reading level Description Reading strategies Reader's Capabilities
Local reading To comprehend the keywords and ideas in the text separately and the local relationship between ideas. The reader is involved only in local and superficial comprehension processes, establishing meaning relationships between words, comprehending separate ideas on the text and creating relationships between isolated consecutive ideas, in order to give sense to what is read. To remember and to paraphrase ideas in the text. The reader sticks to the words in the text.
Global reading To comprehend the global ideas in the text. The reader is involved only in global processes: extracts the most important ideas from the text and builds relationships between these, in order to comprehend the main idea that integrates all the global ideas. To summarize ideas and to organize them in a scheme.
Elaborative reading To make inferences that go beyond the explicit ideas in the text. This is a deeper reading level, in which the reader can go beyond the ideas written in the text, and build inferences, solve new problems and modify the previous knowledge. To think with the text, not to read it only.
Critical reading To comprehend, evaluate and to contrast perspective on a problem. The reader recognizes that texts are never neutral or objective, but they reflect a particular perspective of the world and exclude other. There is an evaluation and comparison of different perspectives in order to build a new one. To distinguish, evaluate and to create a personal viewpoint.

The researcher must be able to do, first, a local reading in order to screen for ideas; and then, at the last stage, a critical reading to extract ideas and contrast them. Therefore several readings may be necessary before writing a theoretical framework.

4.3 Theoretical Framework Construction After a deep review of the literature, you can know if what you are intending to research already has a wide theoretical basis, or if you have to come to a new theoretical perspective from knowledge obtained from several theories. The strategies employed for the construction of your theoretical framework will depend in either case.

  • Adopt an existing theory
    • Because there was plenty of literature found, the problem has been adressed or researched several times before.
    • You must try to always address the problem from a different and novel perspective, to avoid researching what is already known.
  • Setting forth a new theorical perspective
  • The literature found hints that there is not too much available information on the topic, and that the theories found are fuzzily related.
  • You can choose one of the found theories as basis, and relate it with other complementary ideas also found during the literature review. In this way, you can come to creating a new theorical perspective of the situation. The theoretical framework must be understood as a mean for explaining and situating the research problem in a given context, to set a “state of the art” of the research problem fundamental concepts.

5.1 Variables and Types of Relationships A variable is a representation of any phenomenon that can take any value in order to dimension it. For example, in physics the letter T stands for temperature, and this variable can take different values (measured in Celsius, Fahrenheit or Kelvin degrees) in order to represent a physical reality. There are several ways of classifying variables, and this classification depends on the relationships they form with other variables and with the research.

  • Dependent variables, as their name suggest, depend on independent variables. For example, if a hypothesis states that “death rates increase with alcohol consumption”, the dependent variable will be the death rates, since it depends on the rates of alcohol consumption.
  • Independent variables do not depend in any other variable and they can be active or attributive variables. Active variables can be manipulated by the researcher, whereas attributive variables cannot. In the previous example, the alcohol consumption is an attributive variable since it is inherent to the studied population. If the researcher could assign consumption rates to test different hypothesis, it would be an active variable.
  • Strange variables. They can influence the dependent variables. They can be controllable, for example, when the researcher assign the values (sex, age, social condition of the alcohol consumers, etcetera.); or random variables, which values arise from random situations not controlled by the researchers.
  • Mediation or moderation variables. They are related with dependent and independent variables, mediating their relationship and sometimes masking or affecting this relationship. It is very important to identify the research variables of the studied problem in order to make a hypothesis based on one of them. For example, if you are going to analyze society’s opinion about people with tattoos, you may consider as research variables sex, age, social conditions, and education. These variables can affect the opinion about this topic, that is, people’s opinion may be affected by their age, sex or any other demographic variables. It is difficult to investigate about a certain matter considering several points of view, so the research topic has to be reduced to the least possible variables, and the rest of the variables must be in controlled conditions.

5.2 Types of Hypothesis As stated before, hypothesis are possible solutions or explanations to a given research problem. They can set a simple description of what is happening; for example, the hypothesis “in this lake there is a pollutant that has severe consequences on aquatic life”; or express a relation between the situation and a possible solution, for instance, “discharge controls can reduce the concentration levels of the pollutant in water”. This previous examples are conceptual hypothesis. Operational hypothesis occur when including the conceptual hypothesis and the variables involved. Statistical hypothesis are those which build up a mathematical expression. In this context, you can introduce the null and the alternative hypothesis. The null hypothesis is the “main hypothesis”, the one that is being tested. The alternative hypothesis, as the name suggests, becomes a possible consequence if the null hypothesis fails to be proven. Imagine the following null hypothesis: students from public schools obtain the same grades as students from private schools. If you prove that this is not true, then you would have to take the alternative hypothesis: the students from public schools score better than those from private schools or the other way around. Depending on which score results higher during the data collection, is the alternative hypothesis chosen. On the other hand, qualitative research employs open hypothesis, especially for observational research designs. For interpretation work, the term work hypothesis is used. So, depending on the kind of research that will be done and on the identified variables, the researcher must choose a specific hypothesis.

5.3 Hypothesis Definition and Prediction

A hypothesis and a prediction are not the same. A hypothesis is an explanation of a phenomenon, while the prediction is the logical consequence of the hypothesis; that is, the expectation derived from the hypothesis. Hypothesis can be tested, falsifiable (proven to be false) and concrete. The following examples illustrate the differences between a hypothesis and a prediction.

Hypothesis Prediction (in consequence)
You hypothesized that immediate feedback would increase long-term retention of course material. You predicted that students given immediate feedback on course exams would show higher final-exam performance compared to students given feedback two days later.
You hypothesized that, owing to its effects on the frontal-lobe and hippocampus, alcohol consumption would impair long-term memory. You predicted that subjects who drank six or more ounces of alcohol would recall significantly fewer names than students given a placebo beverage.

Hypothesis may be useful to predict phenomena in nature, society, populations, bodies, or other parts of the world. If confirmed or proved to be true they will add applicable knowledge to science, which is the final purpose of scientific research.
